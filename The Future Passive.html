The Future Passive Voice

Course: Grammar for High School Students

What is the Passive Voice?

In English grammar, sentences can be in either active or passive voice.

Active Voice

The subject of the sentence performs the action.

Example: The government will build a new bridge.

Passive Voice

The subject of the sentence receives the action.

Example: A new bridge will be built by the government.

When is the Passive Voice Used?

  • When the person or thing doing the action (the agent) is unknown.
    Example: My motorbike was stolen. (We don't know who stole it.)
  • When the agent is unimportant or obvious.
    Example: The rubbish is collected every morning. (It's obviously collected by sanitation workers.)
  • To create a more formal or objective tone, common in scientific and official reports.
    Example: The samples were analyzed in the lab.
  • To put emphasis on the object or the action itself, rather than who did it.
    Example: The Angkor Wat temple complex was built over many centuries. (The focus is on the temple, not the specific builders.)

Future Passive in a Cambodian Context

Here are some examples of the future simple passive that you might hear in Cambodia.

➤ The new Siem Reap-Angkor International Airport will be expanded further in the coming years.

➤ More traditional Apsara dance shows will be scheduled for the tourist high season.

➤ Local crafts from the Old Market will be sold online to a global audience.

Future Passive Tenses: Forms & Examples

1. Future Simple Passive

will + be + past participle

This is the most common future passive form. It describes an action that will be received by the subject at some point in the future.

  • The new road will be completed next month.
  • An email with the details will be sent to all staff.
  • The hotel room will be cleaned before you arrive.

2. Future Continuous Passive

Rarely Used

will + be + being + past participle

This tense describes an action that will be in progress at a specific time in the future. It is grammatically correct but often sounds awkward and is very uncommon in everyday speech.

  • At 8 PM tonight, the main stage will be being prepared for the concert.
  • This time tomorrow, the final exam papers will be being marked by the teachers.

3. Future Perfect Passive

will + have + been + past participle

This tense describes an action that will be completed before a specific point in the future.

  • By the end of the year, the entire project will have been finished.
  • By the time you get here, dinner will have been cooked.
  • All the tickets will have been sold by Friday.

4. Future Perfect Continuous Passive

Almost Never Used

will + have + been + being + past participle

This tense is grammatically possible but is extremely rare and cumbersome. You will likely never need to use it, but it is included for completeness. It describes an action that has been in progress up to a point in the future.

  • By next month, the bridge will have been being built for two years. (It's much more natural to say: "By next month, they will have been building the bridge for two years.")
